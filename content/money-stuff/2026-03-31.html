The Leveraged Buyout That Ate Itself

There's a special kind of financial poetry when a leveraged buyout target turns out to have been leveraging itself the whole time. Meridian Global Holdings, the $18 billion industrial conglomerate that Apollo and KKR were jointly pursuing, disclosed yesterday that roughly $3.2 billion of its reported EBITDA came from a series of increasingly creative sale-leaseback transactions that, upon closer inspection, look more like secured borrowing than actual operational earnings.

The deal consortium has reportedly paused its financing commitments while advisors at Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley work through the implications. One person familiar with the matter described the mood as "somewhere between confused and furious."

The thing about creative accounting is that it's only creative until someone reads the footnotes. Then it's just accounting.

Meridian's CFO resigned this morning, which is the corporate equivalent of a fire alarm — it doesn't tell you where the fire is, but it strongly suggests there is one.

Treasury Yields and the Art of Not Panicking

The 10-year Treasury yield touched 5.12% this morning before settling back to 5.08%, continuing its slow grind higher that has made the past three months deeply uncomfortable for anyone with duration exposure. The proximate cause was a hotter-than-expected PCE print, but the real story is the ongoing structural repricing of term premium.

What's interesting is how orderly it's been. In 2023, the move from 4% to 5% caused genuine market stress — bank failures, money market disruptions, the works. This time around, the same level has been met with something closer to resigned acceptance. Markets have learned to live with higher rates, which is both reassuring and slightly concerning, because complacency has its own risks.

The Fed meets next week, and futures are pricing essentially zero chance of a cut. The dot plot from the March meeting suggested two cuts later this year, but the market has decided not to believe that, and honestly, the market might be right.

Crypto ETFs and the Institutional Flood

Bitcoin spot ETFs have now accumulated over $120 billion in assets under management, making them collectively the fastest-growing ETF category in history. The latest entrant, a physically-settled Ethereum ETF from Fidelity, gathered $4 billion in its first week.

The institutional adoption narrative has essentially been proven correct, which creates an interesting dynamic: the thing that made crypto exciting to its original proponents (decentralization, independence from traditional finance) is being diluted by the thing that made it investable to institutions (regulation, custody solutions, familiar fund structures).

The purists are unhappy. The portfolio managers are delighted. The lawyers are billing.
