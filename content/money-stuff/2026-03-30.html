The IPO Window Is Open, Bring an Umbrella

Three major tech IPOs priced this week, and the results tell you everything about the current state of public markets: one priced above range and popped 30%, one priced at the midpoint and traded flat, and one priced below range and is currently down 15%. The market is open for business, but it's selective in a way that rewards profitability and punishes "we'll figure out the business model later."

Cerebras Systems, the AI chip company, was the star performer, pricing at $42 and closing its first day at $54.60. The company is profitable, growing at 200% year-over-year, and sells actual products to actual customers — a combination that has become strangely revolutionary in the tech IPO market.

The era of "revenue is a vanity metric" appears to be over. We've entered the era of "revenue is a thing you should have."

Regional Banks: A Quiet Recovery

The KBW Regional Banking Index is up 22% year-to-date, which nobody seems to be talking about, possibly because "regional banks are fine now" is not a headline that generates clicks. But it's worth noting because the regional banking crisis of 2023 was a genuine systemic scare, and the recovery has been driven by actual fundamentals rather than sentiment.

Net interest margins have stabilized as deposit competition has eased, loan losses have remained manageable despite the commercial real estate concerns that dominated 2024, and several regionals have used the past two years to meaningfully improve their liquidity and capital positions.

The lesson, as always: banking crises end, usually more quietly than they begin.

Private Credit's Governance Problem

The $1.7 trillion private credit market is facing its first real governance test as two major borrowers — a healthcare services company and a mid-market software firm — have simultaneously triggered covenant violations on loans held by multiple private credit funds. The interesting part is not the defaults themselves, which are modest in size, but the discovery that different funds hold different tranches of the same credits with conflicting covenant packages.

In the old world of syndicated loans, there were established protocols for intercreditor disputes. In the new world of private credit, everyone assumed they were the senior lender, and nobody checked the other guy's paperwork.

Restructuring lawyers are about to have a very good quarter.

Chart of the Day

Global M&A volume hit $1.2 trillion in Q1 2026, the highest first-quarter total since 2021. The rebound is concentrated in three sectors: technology (driven by AI consolidation), healthcare (driven by GLP-1 supply chain acquisitions), and financial services (driven by everyone buying everyone else's wealth management division). Cross-border deals account for 35% of volume, up from 28% a year ago.

The deal boom is real, but it comes with a caveat: regulatory approval times have roughly doubled since 2020, meaning that a significant portion of announced deals are still in limbo. The pipeline is full; the exit is narrow.
